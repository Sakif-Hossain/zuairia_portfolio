The Rake, the Libertine, and Their (Dis)abilities in Fantomina and The School for Scandal

The rake and the libertine are two figures of elite masculinity that occupied pivotal roles in the culture and literary history of the eighteenth century. Both archetypes often diverged from the accepted social norms of the period and lived unrestrained lives. These terms were often used interchangeably as pejoratives to describe men who practiced little to no sexual restraint or moral responsibility. However, this essay will draw distinctions between the rake and the libertine by examining their representation in Eliza Haywood’s Fantomina; or, Love in a Maze, and Richard Brinsley Sheridan’s The School for Scandal. This close analysis will argue that the titular heroine in Fantomina is an abled female rake, although she displays libertine qualities as well, and Beauplaisir personifies a disabled rake, defying accepted gender norms of the period. This paper will also argue that Charles Surface in The School for Scandal has the outward appearance of a libertine, but is a character who captures the sociopolitical tensions of the eighteenth century as the society transitions from the Stuart to the Hanoverian era. By emphasizing the differences between the rake’s charismatic appeal and the libertine’s philosophical rebellion, this analysis aims to underscore the eighteenth century’s gender dynamics.

Many of the characteristics possessed by the rake and the libertine overlap, such as their shared inclination toward sexual gratification. However, upon analyzing and comparing the characters of Charles Surface and Beauplaisir, the distinctions between both archetypes become vivid. Throughout The School for Scandal, Charles is addressed numerous times as “that libertine” (Sheridan 974), “wild, extravagant, young rogue” (986), and “profligate” (979). Charles’ reputation of behaving recklessly and misspending his inheritance on parties (986) sharply juxtaposes that of his seemingly virtuous brother, Joseph Surface (979). Joseph pretends to be a moral and virtuous gentleman but is a lying hypocrite (1005). The contrast between the Surface brothers sets up the disparity between appearance and reality, the central theme of the play. While Charles’ extravagant lifestyle undeniably places him in precarious situations such as bankruptcy, his generosity and kindness often go unnoticed. One could argue that his benevolence toward Mr. Stanley (Sheridan 998) and his refusal to “part with” Sir Oliver’s portrait (979) symbolize an intrinsic honesty, understanding, and loyalty that are not commonly seen in rakes or libertines. Unlike most rakes and libertines, Charles also does not demonstrate an excessive desire for sexual gratification. Instead, he displays virtue when he refrains from taking Maria’s surname at the party to avoid identifying her, despite his friends’ insistence (Sheridan 992). This act of sentimentality reveals that his admiration for Maria may be more compassionate than carnal, and although he may appear a libertine or rake, his actions demonstrate deep-seated respect for women, setting him apart from the stereotypical traits of the archetypes. The dissimilarities between Charles’ characteristics and the libertine’s common traits could be due to the Hanoverian succession, when George I assumed the throne of Great Britain in 1714, and the social reform began (Robertson). Daniel Gustafson clarifies that as Great Britain withstood the transition from the Stuart to the Hanoverian era, moral principles, and social norms drastically transformed. The rake and the libertine archetypes also underwent significant changes (Gustafson 359). Inspired by absolutism and authority (361), the Stuart rake had “an unrestrained personal will” that was often in competition with the Hanoverian preference for the “emerging ideals of the polite gentleman” (362). During the Hanoverian cultural reform, “the Restoration rake [underwent] a progress of teleological decline” (362) but persisted on stage through George Etherege’s Restoration comedies (Gustafson 370). The liminal but definite existence of the rake during this political period is reflected in Charles’ behavior; he is neither the polite, responsible, moral Hanoverian gentleman nor the experienced, womanizing, misogynistic Stuart absolutist. Aptly embodying the impact of the sociopolitical transition, Charles effectively reveals the changing dynamics of gender and sexuality in the eighteenth century.

Although Charles captures the sociopolitical tensions and societal transitions of the eighteenth century, the resemblance between him and a libertine often causes others to misunderstand him and reveals their limited understanding of what constitutes a true libertine. The townspeople’s negative reactions toward Charles’ outwardly libertine behavior suggest that the moral values of sensibility, balance, restraint, order, decorum, and rationality were highly regarded in the eighteenth century, while attitudes – such as that of Charles – that diverged from such neoclassical values were scorned (Robertson). One could contend that through Charles, Sheridan attempts to solve the eighteenth century’s misunderstandings regarding elite masculinities, declaring that what is outwardly presented is not necessarily genuine. The courtier and poet John Wilmot, 2nd Earl of Rochester, is described by Lisa O’Connell as the most notable Restoration libertine, whose “dashing and extravagant persona” inspired various dramatic characters in court-licensed theatres in London after 1660 (O’Connell 219). Libertinage emerged with the influence of the Italian satirist Pietro Aretino and the English philosopher Thomas Hobbes (220-224). Aretino’s argument about sex is a natural phenomenon “deployed obscenity against established authority” (221) and inspired the creation of the amatory fiction genre that was later dominated by female authors like Haywood (222). Additionally, because Hobbes rejected political and religious dogma, a way of life that was withdrawn from society was considered “a condition of living well” (O’Connell 222), promoting the libertine’s philosophical, irreligious, and intellectually provocative behavior that broadly defied morality and authority. Consequently, it can be concluded that regardless of the libertine’s indulgence in sexual gratification and extravagance, they were philosophically rebellious, and their greater purpose was to defy the established moral and religious structures. Their engagement in sexual conquests did not seek the immediate, personal gratification that the rakes pursued. As Charles does not philosophically challenge society, or have an insatiable hunger for sexual pursuits, he is not reminiscent of the libertine or the rake. Charles’ sentiments, honesty, and kindness do not conform to the defiance and rebellion that a libertine with “Hobbist modes” (O’Connell 228) would demonstrate, or the sexual hunger an eighteenth-century rake would crave (O’Connell 228-229). There is, therefore, a strong claim to be made that through Charles, Sheridan ultimately exposes how one could have a truly sensible heart underneath their outwardly libertine behavior. Sheridan also uses Charles to uncover how interwoven the traits of rakish and libertine masculinities are, and how these overlapping traits may have obscured the eighteenth-century audience’s understanding of the distinctions between the archetypes.

Unlike Charles, Beauplaisir possesses more womanizing and rakish qualities as an individual who lacks loyalty and commitment. With his name translating into “beautiful pleasure” in French (Robertson), his character aptly embodies rakish masculinity. Sexual gratification is clearly crucial to Beauplaisir, but so is the act of engaging with multiple women, because the longer he stays with one lady, the more “tasteless and insipid” she appears to him (Haywood 636), not to mention that he enjoys taking the “liberty to pursue new conquests” (637) to enrich his collection of “highly rapturous” victories (635). Beauplaisir’s desire-driven nature evokes readers to critique his inability to commit to long-term relationships, exposing how rakes grow weary of women once their novelty fades (Robertson). By assigning such predictable behavioral patterns to Beauplaisir’s rakish character, Haywood ridicules the male gaze, which compels women to reinvent themselves continually if they are to maintain a man’s attention (Robertson). Beauplaisir’s lack of loyalty and commitment strikingly contrasts with Fantomina’s unwavering determination to sustain his affection, which not only is a demonstration of her emotional complexity but also serves as a testament to her intellectually challenging and manipulating the societal norms of the period (Robertson). Beauplaisir’s limited emotional depth further comments on how an eighteenth-century rake’s need for physical fulfillment overrules morality, courteousness, and consideration; Beauplaisir “[compels Celia] to sit in his lap” and “[devours] her lips… with greedy kisses,” without acknowledging that she was “half-reluctant” to his behavior and did not fully consent to such intimacy (Haywood 638). This indifference to the absence of consent during sexual activities acts as a microcosm of the eighteenth century’s social constructs regarding gender hierarchies, which promised men like Beauplaisir – rakish or not – absolute freedom of movement but imposed limitations on women like Fantomina.

Although Haywood gives Beauplaisir ultimate freedom in Fantomina, one could contend that she purposely uses him as a narrative tool to highlight Fantomina’s rakish, or, borderline libertine femininity. One could interpret Beauplaisir’s predictability as the leverage that allows Fantomina to experiment with her identity and autonomy because she calculates his moves before he contemplates them (Robertson). Upon noting “[Beauplaisir’s] inconstancy and levity of nature” (Haywood 641), she plans “another project… which she soon [ripens] into action” (641). Although notably naïve at the beginning of the play (Robertson), Fantomina’s increasing adaptability and mastery of theatrics with each disguise marks her resilience. Based on this premise, one could assert that the ability to possess such tremendous mental faculties in a society that was known to be patriarchal almost transforms Fantomina into a libertine, who, at a more profound level, was philosophically rebelling against the long-established constraints imposed on women. Through her unyielding pursuit of sexual desires and agency, she not only escapes the limitations of her “distinguished birth” (632) but also demonstrates that women in the eighteenth century were just as capable of defying morality and intellectually challenging society as their male counterparts. Consequently, Haywood’s use of Beauplaisir as a narrative device inspires more female agency and resilience within the patriarchal, eighteenth-century society. For such a remarkable philosophical rebellion, it would be natural to classify Fantomina as a libertine. However, her unfaltering sexual appetite aligns her more closely with the rake. Despite feeling “deceived and cheated [by Beauplaisir]” and having no “real tenderness for him,” she proceeds to disguise herself as Incognita to continue her scheme (641) instead of bringing it to an end (Robertson). Fantomina’s attempts to extend her plot provoke one to consider that the “luxurious gratification of [her] wild desires” (640) is of utmost importance to her. Until she is prevented by moral authority – in essence, her mother – her carnal urges take precedence over the larger battle she has against eighteenth-century society, as well as her self-respect and dignity. By not allowing Fantomina to fully transform into a female libertine, Haywood solidifies the differences between the rake and the libertine and questions the eighteenth-century society for granting men space to experiment with their gender dynamics but punishing women for the same desires.

The challenges in categorizing Fantomina as a female rake underscores the idea that gender and sexuality in the eighteenth century operated on a spectrum, rather than fitting neatly into the binary categories of the rake and the libertine. Often lost in such rigid categorizations is the case of the literary character’s ability, or lack thereof, to adhere to and fulfill the needs and expectations of these archetypes. Difeng Chueh considers an often-overlooked aspect of libertine masculinity in the eighteenth century – its relation to disability. Through his close reading of Fantomina, Chueh concludes that Beauplaisir’s observation skills are central to his libertinism. With these skills – from offering the “usual salutations” to Fantomina at the playhouse (Haywood 633) to altering his behavior to seduce Celia (638) – Beauplaisir perfects his wooing tactics (Chueh 3). Chueh further asserts that the observation skills that make Beauplaisir a libertine ironically also become the qualities that discredit his status (3-4). Quoting Samuel Johnson’s fifth definition of the term “disable” – which is “to exclude as wanting proper qualifications” – Chueh asserts that Beauplaisir’s abled rake status begins to fail as the heroine advances in her plot because he loses his observation skills (Chueh 4). This argument about the reversal of power between Beauplaisir and Fantomina is persuasive, but Chueh’s classification of Beauplaisir as a libertine is not. To problematize this interpretation of Beauplaisir’s masculinity, libertines, in the words of Johnson, are those who are “unconfined,” “[live] without restraint or law,” and “[pay] no regard to the precepts of religion” (Johnson). Based on Johnson’s definition, one could counter Chueh, because, despite Beauplaisir’s sexual endeavors with those seemingly below his station, Beauplaisir respects law and religion; he does not trespass the boundaries of politeness and morality when he courts the Lady in her “drawing room” and refrains from “using with her that freedom” he would with a prostitute (Haywood 633). In this manner, Beauplaisir adheres more to religion and morality than rakes traditionally do and could be classified as a disabled rake who fails to meet the expectations of his fellow rakish, womanizing, amorous peers.

Additionally, the argument about observation skills being a marker of rakish identity explicates Fantomina’s success as an abled female rake and Charles’s failure as both a rake and libertine. According to Johnson, rakes are “loose, disorderly, vicious, wild, gay, thoughtless” and “addicted to pleasure” (Johnson). The last definition is especially resonant with Fantomina’s unwavering hypersexuality. While Fantomina and Beauplaisir both share a common desire to indulge in sexual gratification, she adheres to her rakish femininity more thoroughly than Beauplaisir does to his rakish masculinity. By repeatedly managing to trick Beauplaisir and always staying ahead of him, she inadvertently displays richer observation skills than him. Beauplaisir’s failure to recognize Fantomina’s disguises strikingly contrast with Fantomina’s adaptability and “qualifications,” which, according to Johnson’s definition of disability (Chueh), would license her as an abled rake. In this spectrum of the rake against the libertine, it seems reasonable to conclude that Beauplaisir clings to his disabled rake status, and Fantomina is an abled female rake, though she maintains some libertine qualities too. Conversely, Charles’ observation skills are poorer than Fantomina’s or Beauplaisir’s. Viewing people and situations only at face value, Charles’ lack of inquiry into others’ motives often makes him appear naïve (Robertson). Not only does he fail to recognize Joseph’s superficial character (Sheridan 1004), but he also does not think it necessary to cross-examine Mr. Stanley’s background (998). Charles’ inability to discern Joseph’s duplicitous nature and failure to understand that anyone could have been posing as Mr. Stanley to financially exploit him further demonstrates his lack of observation skills, which could hypothetically eliminate his status as a rake or libertine, had he occupied either role.

To conclude, Fantomina and The School for Scandal challenge the defined and specified understanding of gender, revealing how characters may embody rakish or libertine characteristics to varying extents. The distinctions drawn in this essay aim to highlight that those who are true rakes or libertines also possess certain qualities that allow them to meet the needs and expectations of their role. Conversely, those who merely perform or hold the outward appearance of a rake or libertine may not necessarily possess those attributes, signifying the obscurity and complexity of eighteenth-century gender dynamics. This close analysis invites further consideration of how female literary characters embodied libertine femininity, and the impact of their endeavors on eighteenth-century women.
