A Musical Awakening: Loss, Loyalty, and Legacy in Rang De Basanti

The ideological synchronicity between Indian youth and historical revolutionaries is a central theme in Rakeysh Omprakash Mehra’s Rang De Basanti, hereinafter RDB. By dissolving the boundaries between the personal and the political, RDB revitalizes transgenerational relationships. Drawing on Sikata Banerjee’s discussion of muscular nationalism and Charu Gupta’s exploration of maternal metaphors, I argue that political awakening is less a product of formal education and more a result of lived experiences shaped by personal loss and inherited legacy. Particularly, I will focus on the film’s soundtrack, an often-overlooked narrative device that follows the protagonists’ transition from political apathy to enlightenment.

Mehra critiques political indifference and institutionalized education with the satirical “Masti Ki Paathshaala” (RDB 16:10). The protagonists’ repeated declaration of “loose control” and “I’m a rebel” (20:08-26) highlights their dismissal of formal education and defiance of societal norms. Their rebellion critiques the value of institutionalized education that does not prepare the youth for real-world challenges, a concern DJ raises with Sue (45:53-47:20). Instead, the protagonists engage in a communal and experiential approach to learning. This critique aligns with contemporary debates about outdated educational models, which can neglect emotional intelligence and critical engagement. Although typical for young university students, Aslam’s sarcastic remark about “studying” being an accusation instead of a privilege (16:55-17:02) suggests that formal education falls short in leaving a lasting impression on students and equipping them with skills that will help them understand their social responsibilities. Karan’s admission in the nightclub that he cannot relate to India’s freedom fighters further amplifies the youth’s detachment from their national history (RDB 89:33-90:27). By embedding a critique of traditional education in the satirical yet reflective “Masti Ki Paathshaala,” Mehra pushes us to question whether traditional education fosters empathy and critical engagement necessary to tackle societal challenges, and if we have what it takes to retaliate against unjust politics.

Using “Luka Chuppi,” Mehra underscores personal loss (RDB 106:23-110:28) as one of the factors that can shatter political apathy. This duet between a mourning Aishwarya and a deceased Ajay juxtaposes maternal grief with the bittersweet liberation of martyrdom. With the evening approaching and Aishwarya’s eyesight blurring (107:00-15), a deeper sense of grief arrives, metaphorically symbolizing a mother’s instinctive but futile attempts to connect with her deceased child. In response to Aishwarya’s requests, Ajay describes his newfound liberation, which comes at the cost of detachment from his loved ones (109:50-110:20). His sacrifice is reflected in the dichotomy of the song’s lyrics, where his lines – sung by A.R. Rahman – introduce new developments, but Aishwarya’s lines – sung by Lata Mangeshkar – are consistently unresponsive to the details mentioned. The dissonance between Aishwarya’s static refrain and Ajay’s evolving diction stiffens this unbridgeable, one-sided conversation where Aishwarya remains woefully unaware of Ajay’s replies. This disconnection between the living and the dead, and Aishwarya’s personal grief, project on a national scale as she embodies “Mother India,” who is mourning for her lost sons (Harrington 6). Gupta’s assertion that the maternal metaphor unifies citizens under nationalist ideals (4291) is evident in the sentimental appeal of RDB, as all protagonists feel a sense of obligation and protectiveness toward Aishwarya. While I agree with Gupta’s claim that the misuse of such symbolisms by right-wing Hindu politicians can impose ultranationalism, especially implicating marginalized populations (4292), I argue that such associations also help citizens forge genuine connections with their lands. It is instinctive not only for men but everyone, to protect and care for their parents. If the nation is metaphorically a mother, then its citizens are her children. This symbolism thus makes social issues a family matter, ultimately inspiring the public to be more involved in society. However, with RDB and many other films, Bollywood has overused the “Mother India” trope, diluting its potency (Harrington 6). This raises questions about the violence portrayed by the protagonists, whether it truly brings justice for Ajay, or if it is their way to mask personal vendetta in the name of patriotism. Despite the male protagonists’ sacrifices, Ajay cannot return, and some relationships, such as Aishwarya’s and Ajay’s, will remain severed forever. This bleak representation of grief prompts us to consider if the outcome for Ajay would be different if the protagonists had raised their voices against the MiG-21 jet plane scam when they first learned about it instead of behaving apathetically (RDB 43:13-26). Taking Ajay’s death into consideration, I argue that Mehra aims to evoke a sense of guilt in his viewers, critiquing society’s tendency to remain politically disengaged until they experience consequences personally, and underscoring how much we can lose if we do not fight.

While personal and familial sacrifices are pivotal in promoting political consciousness, Mehra also demonstrates the significance of inherited legacy in “Lalkaar” (RDB 95:15-97:39). Popularised by Ram Prasad Bismil, the revolutionary Laxman reenacts, “Lalkaar” explicitly binds the actions of the young protagonists to the ideals of the historical revolutionaries. As the protagonists claim to “wear a shroud” (96:55-97:12), they mirror the ethos of their predecessors who also accepted martyrdom. This imagery of death in “Lalkaar” creates a sense of continuity in the values that drive revolutionary movements across generations. The declaration of “revolution in [their] veins” captures their ancestors’ collective passion (97:14-17), reinforcing that individual lives are transient compared to the lasting impact of revolutionary action. Even though it takes them a while, the protagonists in RDB understand their role as the next link in the chain of resistance and change. We could argue that it is not possible to inherit the legacy of those who are not tied to us by blood, but Mehra implores us to view RDB as the story of a group of friends who metaphorically inherit the historical revolutionaries’ patriotism by reenacting their struggles. “Lalkaar” also features a reenactment of the peaceful protest against the Simon Commission (96:50), which is recreated during the candle march for Ajay in “Khoon Chala” (111:50), metaphorically intertwining the dimensions of the past and present. However, the protagonists not only inherit their ancestors’ political ideology, but their desire for personal vengeance as well. Just as the historical revolutionaries avenged the death of Lala Lajpat Rai (97:39), a father figure, by assassinating James Scott (98:23-99:15), the protagonists retaliate against the Defence Minister for Ajay’s death (RDB 128:40-129:58), with Aishwarya standing as their moral anchor. The synchronicity in the actions of the historical revolutionaries and the protagonists thus reinforces that our present-day identities are the products of our ancestors’ struggles for justice. Rochona Majumdar’s comment about songs unifying emotions and memories across temporal divides aptly registers in RDB (109) because Mehra uses the soundtrack to connect the past and present. Through such portrayal of transgenerational relationships, Mehra attempts to redefine patriotism as a sense of loyalty, not only towards one’s country but also towards one’s family, blood-related or not. Mehra’s portrayal of patriotic legacy also urges us to consider what we define as patriotism, and if the loyalty we feel for our families extends to our nations as well.

However, Mehra’s definition of patriotism raises questions about exclusion. Banerjee critiques India’s, and accordingly Mehra’s, reliance on “muscular nationalism” as a symbol of global triumphalism, which finds vivid expression in the male protagonists’ ultimate sacrifice (2-3). By demonstrating this androcentric climax, Mehra risks exhibiting exclusionary nationalism, disseminating the idea that women are not allowed to be national heroes. Reiterating Mehra’s representation of “Mother India,” we could also argue that he unintentionally illustrates the metaphorical mother nation favoring the sacrifices of her sons while rejecting the dedication of her daughters. Contrary to Banerjee’s claims, I do not believe Sonia merely “[begs] her friends to avenge Ajay’s death” or “remains on the side-lines” (67). Considering the political neutrality Sonia initially maintains, it is notable that in her timeline, she suggests murder as punishment (RDB 127:35-53), mirroring Durgawati Devi’s boldness. Additionally, if a comatose Aishwarya is truly “reminiscent of the suffering motherland” (Banerjee 65) then certainly as her caretaker in the hospital (RDB 117:10-30), Sonia emerges as an unwavering and committed patriot. Aishwarya defeats the frail characteristics of her archetype as well; during the candlelight vigil in “Khoon Chala,” Sonia and Aishwarya are not alarmed by the baton charge, unlike the other protagonists (114:00-15). However, we could also argue that they are primarily driven by the movement because it concerns Ajay. It is therefore questionable if they would still act persistently had it been another soldier in Ajay’s place. This reinstates Mehra’s critique of reactive activism, which is driven almost exclusively by personal loss, and his emphasis on sustained, dedicated engagement with political matters.

Pavitra Sundar’s concept of the “deejay aesthetic” encapsulates Mehra’s use of music to “remix” fragments of the past so that it is relatable to present-day youth. DJ’s name, symbolically linked to this aesthetic, underscores the film’s intent to retell historical narratives dynamically (Sundar). Mehra’s reimagination of history provides viewers with another opportunity to relearn, reengage, and reinterpret history as a part of their identity that demands critical engagement, rather than simply accepting it as foreign, distant, and superficial facts. In essence, Rang De Basanti urges us to redefine patriotism, bridging the past and present through acts of sustained political engagement. Mehra’s call for continual engagement reminds us that our nations are eagerly waiting for us to act so that we can protect our loved ones. As we step into the future, we must accept our social responsibility of ensuring that the legacy we leave behind honors not only our ancestors but our descendants as well.
