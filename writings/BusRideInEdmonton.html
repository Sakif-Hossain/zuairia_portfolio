A Bus Ride In Edmonton: A Mosaic of Stories

A bus ride in Edmonton is a mosaic of stories.

A mother rushes to get her son to school on time, balancing a steaming cup of espresso while her other hand clings to her toddler’s small, warm fingers. She tries to shake off the remnants of sleep with her frequent, hurried sips. As her mind spins with the thoughts of forgotten homework, she frantically checks her son’s backpack for one last time. As they approach their stop, she quickly gathers her son’s backpack and lunch bag and walks with him to the school entrance. She kneels to kiss his forehead, sending him off with an “I love you” and the quiet reassurance, “Mommy’s so proud of you.”

As the mother and son duo disappear into the crowd, a young man rushes onto the bus, breathless and flushed. He adjusts his headset and grabs onto the nearest rail as a gravely ill patient clings to life. His gaze drifts to the exit sign as he gets lost in thoughts that seem as endless as the road ahead. He is jolted out of his thoughts as more backpacks squeeze in and shove him into a corner. As he struggles to find even the smallest bit of room, he grips another rail, clenching his fists to avoid the irritated glances from the seated passengers.

The seated passengers are all fascinating creatures. A middle-aged blonde woman, vibrant and full of life, sits next to a girl who appears to be a university student and elaborates on her time living in a downtown Vancouver trailer. As she broadcasts her street life struggles and the petty battles fought over scraps of food, the student listens politely, occasionally nodding and smiling to keep up the pretense of interest.

Seated behind them, an elderly man is lost in a different era — the 1970s — when paper maps were still around. “I drove to every corner, correcting the maps before anyone else could,” he boasts to his neighbor, who listens with eyebrows raised in awe. In sheer amazement, his neighbor lets out a “wow” as he lists the discrepancies in today’s Google Maps. As they shake hands in parting, a newfound trust forms between them.

As some passengers smile in awe at this new friendship, others turn their curious heads to the loud bunch at the back. Dressed in light blue scrubs and cushioned white shoes, a cluster of nursing students quiz each other, sharing the stress and urgency of their last-minute cramming with the bus and providing everyone with a free lesson on taking medical history. As they prepare to get off at their stop, the murmurs of medical jargon fade.

A bus ride in Edmonton is indeed, a mosaic of stories. From a mother who wants to ensure that her son is not late to school, to a group of last-minute, cramming nursing students who want to pass their midterms, each ride introduces us to many stories and many goals. Though the journey is brief, each bus becomes a vessel for a lot of dreams. And, each ride offers something unique — whether it’s the ability to maintain patience and virtue so that someone else feels comfortable, or the solace in a slowly budding friendship.

When passengers step off at their respective stops, they carry with them a small but significant piece of someone else’s life — as a memento of the journey that lingers long after the ride has ended.

Zuairia Shahrin
