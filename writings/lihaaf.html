A Critical Response: Patriarchal Oppression and Sexual Abuse in “Lihaaf”

Ismat Chughtai’s Lihaaf is a short story that addresses patriarchal oppression and sexual abuse in 20th-century India. Chughtai’s use of the “quilt” as a metaphor, heat, and elephant motifs, and textile symbolism analogizes the Nawab’s exploitation of Begum Jaan as a façade for his homosexuality and Begum Jaan’s subsequent exploitation of Rabbu as a sexual slave. Demonstrating the transmission of psychological trauma down the social hierarchy, Lihaaf exhibits the vicious nature of hypersexuality and power dynamics.

Chughtai establishes the “quilt” metaphor to facilitate our understanding of the trauma the Nawab inflicted on Begum Jaan. Behind a façade of virtue, the Nawab’s attraction to young boys exposed his use of Begum Jaan as a “quilt” that concealed his homosexuality from societal scrutiny. Homosexuality was a taboo in Chughtai’s era, and his marriage protected him from accusations while enabling his sexual desires. The Nawab's behavior led to Begum Jaan feeling neglected, also portrayed through textile symbolism. The Nawab’s pursuit of sheer and translucent “gossamer shirts” symbolizes his lust-driven superficial character. Contrarily, the intricate embroidery in Begum Jaan’s “jaali karga kurtas” symbolizes her complex personality as a wife who was not propelled solely by carnal pleasures, but companionship as well. Begum Jaan’s isolation reflects the neglect many Indian women endured in patriarchal, loveless marriages where their needs were dismissed. Being confined in the Nawab’s house also diminished her existence to that of inanimate, forgotten objects, resembling the suppression and objectification women tolerated in the 1940s.

Chughtai’s use of heat as a central motif shows that the Nawab’s objectification and oppression of Begum Jaan escalates to abuse, which is perpetuated on Rabbu. By maintaining his association with male students, the Nawab repeatedly tortured Begum Jaan, making her “blood boil.” This trauma manifested as sexual abuse towards Rabbu and reduced her status to a mere sexual servant, as she was compelled to fulfill Begum Jaan’s unceasing demands for “special oil massages” and satiate her “itch.” Begum Jaan’s reliance on Rabbu to satisfy her sexual needs under the quilt and her growing irritability in Rabbu’s absence further highlights that she prioritized sexual pleasures over emotional fulfillment–a change induced by the Nawab’s misconduct. Chughtai’s continued employment of the “quilt” metaphor draws further parallels between the Nawab and Begum Jaan as sexual offenders; just as the Nawab used Begum Jaan as his “quilt,” Begum Jaan used Rabbu as hers–a pawn to achieve sexual satisfaction. This parallel also draws similarities between the Nawab and Begum Jaan as lecherous and self-serving oppressors who subjugated their social subordinates.

The elephant motif highlights more correlations between Begum Jaan and the Nawab, enhancing our understanding of her hypersexuality. As Indian social norms often associate elephants with sensuality, the resemblance between her quilt’s shadow and a sexualized animal underscores her carnal urges. However, when juxtaposed with her elegant body, the elephant motif reveals that she uses physical beauty and grace as a façade to mask her hypersexual desires, mirroring the Nawab’s use of her as a cover for his own. Her heightened libido and oppressive nature parallel the patriarchal control that was once imposed on her by the Nawab, and her assertion of the same on Rabbu illustrates the dissemination of trauma through a power dynamic.

Chughtai’s reiteration of the heat motif illustrates the descent of sexual abuse upon the narrator through the social hierarchy. Upon “pressing” the narrator like “a clay doll” and disregarding her bodily autonomy, Begum Jaan reprises her role as a hypersexual oppressor who is aroused even by the narrator’s innocent touch and likes to dominate and objectify her sexually. The narrator’s later realization of the “quilt” as a symbol of her childhood trauma, and Chughtai’s characterization of it as a representation of patriarchal oppression and sexual abuse challenges the social hierarchy of 20th-century India, where the powerful–like the Nawab and Begum Jaan–could exploit the defenseless–such as Rabbu and the narrator–without facing any consequences.

Zuairia Shahrin
