A Critical Response: Remembering the Forgotten in “The Dog of Tetwal”

Saadat Hasan Manto’s “The Dog of Tetwal” is set in the aftermath of the Partition of India, portraying a village in Jammu and Kashmir as a no man’s land where the war is a microcosmic representation of the ongoing conflict between India and Pakistan over Kashmir. The realist fiction focuses on toxic ultranationalism, using the dog as an allegory to explore the identity crisis faced by millions of refugees caught in the futility and absurdity of the Partition.

Manto illustrates the futility of drawing borders by highlighting the similarities between Indians and Pakistanis, suggesting that the Partition was unnecessary. From Jamadar Harnam Singh and Bashir “softly humming” love songs from their respective camps (193) to their shared morning routines, Manto establishes that both sides share strikingly similar habits. However, the most remarkable resemblance was in their use of the Hindustani vernacular to name the dog, as both groups chose onomatopoeically similar phrases – “Chapad Jhunjhun” (191) and “Sapar Sunsun” (196). Neither of the names are standard phrases, but both are playful expressions used to describe sound and rhythmic movement, making them phonetic duplicates. Manto’s revelation of these similarities demonstrates the impracticality of drawing borders between people who share a common language and lifestyle because both sides share enough common interests to coexist peacefully despite their religious differences.

Manto highlights the soldiers’ toxic ultranationalism, a result of Partition, to urge his readers to reconsider the nature of their national identity. As the dog arrives at their respective camps, neither Harnam Singh nor Subedar Himmat Khan can accept it for what it is and are compelled to label it to signify its nationality. This common propensity for assigning binary national identities demonstrates that they saw the innocent animal as an enemy rather than a living being, while also reiterating the striking similarities between Indians and Pakistanis, such that they even share the same animosity towards those who are not their own. Having lost all sense of humanity and respect for life, Harnam Singh and Himmat Khan’s unceasing bullet shots toward the dog at the end of the narrative not only revealed their shared inclination to retaliate when the other attack, but also their common sadistic personalities found the dog’s fear to be “a great deal of pleasure” (198). Manto uses the soldiers’ compassionless behavior toward the dog to mock the concept of extreme nationalism, which is often upheld through performative acts of aggression and implores post-Partition readers to reconsider their understanding of nationalism. Manto also relates this ultranationalism with the absurdity of the Partition by amplifying the innocence of the dog, who, despite being an animal, felt more humanity than the “human” soldiers themselves, because it looked up to them as trustworthy and kind human beings, not as vindictive Indians or Pakistanis. This urges present-day readers to imagine the betrayal the refugees of the Partition might have faced in relying on the soldiers for support and understanding but to no avail.

Manto uses Harnam Singh and Himmat Khan’s shared proclivity for nationalistic labeling to address the identity crisis that affected many refugees during the Partition. Through the rapid naming and renaming of the dog from “Chapad Jhunjhun” (191) to “Sapar Sunsun” (196), Manto establishes identity as a transitory attribute, making a commentary on the fluid identities of the Partition refugees, which included Manto and his family as well. The dog’s metaphorical struggle limping between two identities, and its literal struggle between two borders during its last moments allegorizes the disorientation and displacement of many refugees, who, without any firm identities during the Partition, were going just as astray in real life as the dog does in this realist narrative.

The story’s climax, where the dog is unsafe in India, Pakistan, and even in no man’s land, conveys the situational irony of a war that fails to secure anyone’s safety. Manto deliberately builds this suspense to demonstrate that the Partition only benefitted the authorities who were unwaveringly committed to building extremist, non-secular nations, and pays homage to those who were lost and forgotten – those who “died a dog’s death” (199).

Zuairia Shahrin
